## General Game Rules
- Ace is always high
- You **have** to follow suit
  - if you can't follow suit, you can play **any** card you wish
- You do **not** have to win the trick (ie you can "duck")
- Card rank follows natural order (A beats K beats Q etc)
- The bid total in each round **can not** equal the trick total of the round
- Dealing moves clockwise with each round.
- While dealing, you may deal multiple cards to each player (for example three cards at a time), but each player must get the same number of cards per turn.  Generally you would deal 4 or fewer cards to each player

## Round 1
The first round deals all 52 cards (13 per player).  There is no trump suit.  Each player, starting with the player left of dealer, bids on the number of tricks they expect to win.  Zero is a valid bid unless mathmatically eliminated.  The dealer's bid, when summed with the other bids **can not** equal the number of tricks available in the round (13 in this case).  

For example, if the first bid is 5, the second bid is 4 and the third bid is 3, the bid total is 12.  13 - 12 = 1 so the dealer may not bid 1.  They can bid 0 or any number greater than 1, but not 1.

If the dealer "goes under", there's an extra trick everyone is trying to avoid.  If the dealer goes over, there is a shortage of tricks all players will be fighting over.

After bids are recorded, the player left of dealer leads a card of their choice.  Each player in turn plays a valid card.  Players much follow suit if possible, but they do not have to take the trick.  They may choose to "duck" by playing a lower ranked card.  If the player is void of the led suit, they may play any card of their choice.  After all four cards are played, the winner of the trick collects the four card and places them face down in front of them, such that all players can see how many tricks each player has at all times.

The winner of the trick now leads the next card and play continues to move around the table following the same rules.  The goal being for each player to take **exactly** the number of tricks they bid (ie "making" their bid).

After the last trick is won, totals are compared to bids.  Players get 1 point for every trick taken and a 10 point bonus if they hit their bid.  For example:
- a player that bids 4 and takes 4 gets 14 points
- a player that bids 0 and takes 0 gets 10 points
- a player that bids 3 and takes 2 gets 2 points
- a player that bids 3 and takes 5 gets 5 points

This concludes round 1.  The player to the left of the dealer collects all the cards, shuffles and round 2 starts

## Subsequent Rounds
In each subsequent round, the number of cards dealt to each player decreases by one.  After dealing the appropriate number of cards for the round, from the remaining deck, the top card is flipped and that suit is trump for the round.  This card is left face up for the entire round.

For example
- in round two, each player will get 12 cards leaving 4 unused cards in the deck.  The top of those four cards is turned over to designate the trump suit (now leaving three cards unknown and out of play)
- in round three, each player will get 11 cards leaving 8 usused.  The top one turned over to reveal the trump suit for this round (and leaving 7 cards unknown and out of play)

Game play continues as in round 1, but now when a player cannot follow suit, they may choose to play trump.  Subsequent player are still forced to follow the led suit.  If a player does not have the led suit **they are not required to play trump** (this is known as tossing off).  If a player does play trump, a later player (void of the led suit), can play any card they wish:
- they could over trump to take the trick
- they could burn a trump to avoid getting stuck with it later
- they could play any non-trump offsuit card they wish (usually to avoid getting stuck with it later)

Play continues round by round until round 13 where only one card is dealt to each player.  At the conclusion of the thirteenth round the player with the most points is declared the winner.  This marks the end of the "short" game.  If you've made it this far and enjoyed the game, you can complete the "long" game by playing the levels in reverse back up to 13 cards (only playing the round of 1 card once).

## Tips / Tricks / Hints
If you are just getting started, here's a few tips to help you get your sea legs:
- when faced with a bidding decision, bid less and duck...it's usually easier
- if you are the first player to act in the round, use that to your advantage
- being void of a suit is advantageous
- once you miss your bid (or realize you're going to miss) suck up as many tricks as possible and screw the whole table
- in extreme circumstances, as the lead bidder, you _could_ misrepresent your hand intentionaly to encourage others to over bid and then suck up all the tricks with control of the lead.  Nobody gets their bid, but you take the majority of the tricks (and therefore points)
